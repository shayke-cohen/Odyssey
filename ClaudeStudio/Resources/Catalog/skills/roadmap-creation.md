# Roadmap Creation

## When to Activate

Use when you need a product or engineering roadmap that ties work to strategy, not just a backlog. Activate at quarterly planning, after major strategy shifts, when stakeholders ask “what’s next,” or when delivery feels reactive without clear outcomes.

## Process

1. **Anchor on outcomes** — Write 3–7 measurable outcomes (e.g., activation rate, latency SLO, support volume). Each roadmap item should map to at least one outcome; if it does not, defer or reframe it.
2. **Sequence by dependencies and risk** — Order work so risky or unknown items start early (“fail fast”). Draw a simple dependency graph; put blocking integrations and platform choices before feature polish.
3. **Bucket time horizons** — Use **Now** (committed, next 0–6 weeks), **Next** (likely next quarter, 20–40% confidence), **Later** (directional, exploratory). Label confidence explicitly; avoid fake precision on dates for Next/Later.
4. **Connect to customer evidence** — For each theme, cite interviews, support themes, usage analytics, or sales feedback. Store links in your doc or ticket; avoid “we think users want…” without a source.
5. **Review quarterly** — Archive completed items, re-score outcomes, and move buckets. Compare planned vs. actual to calibrate future horizons.

## Checklist

- [ ] Every item links to an outcome or metric
- [ ] Dependencies and highest-risk bets are front-loaded
- [ ] Now/Next/Later buckets have honest confidence labels
- [ ] Customer or operational evidence is cited per major theme
- [ ] Next review date and owner are named

## Tips

Prefer themes over a flat feature list so teams can swap tactics without rewriting the roadmap. Use a single source of truth (Notion, Linear roadmap view, or a markdown file in repo) and link out to epics in Jira/Linear/GitHub Projects. When leadership pushes for certainty on Later items, offer scenarios (“if X, then Y”) instead of single-date commitments.
